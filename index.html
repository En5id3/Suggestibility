Theory

Laws of Suggestibility

These laws outline principles that help explain how and why people respond to hypnotic suggestions, developed by early hypnotists and psychologists.

01

Law of Concentrated Attention

The more one concentrates on a particular idea or suggestion, the more likely it is to be accepted and acted upon. Focused attention enhances the absorption of the suggestion.

Example

If a hypnotist asks a subject to focus intensely on the idea of their hand feeling warm, the subject's concentrated attention on that sensation can cause the hand to actually feel warmer. The more the subject concentrates, the more likely they are to experience the suggested warmth.

02

Law of Reversed Effect

The harder one tries to do something, the less they are able to do it. This often occurs when conscious effort conflicts with the subconscious will.

Example

When trying to fall asleep, the more you consciously try to sleep, the harder it becomes. Similarly, under hypnosis, if a subject tries too hard to resist a suggestion, they may find it even more difficult to do so.

03

Law of Dominant Effect

A stronger emotion tends to replace a weaker one. By attaching a strong emotion to a suggestion, the suggestion is more likely to be accepted.

Example

A hypnotist might attach a feeling of intense calm and safety to the suggestion of overcoming a phobia. The stronger emotion of safety can override the weaker emotion of fear related to the phobia.

04

Law of Repetition

Repeating a suggestion increases its effectiveness. Repetition helps reinforce the suggestion in the mind, making it more likely to be accepted and acted upon.

Example

A hypnotist might repeatedly suggest relaxation and calm during a session. The more times the suggestion is made, the deeper the relaxation and the more likely the subject is to carry it over into their daily life.

05

Law of Association

When a suggestion is linked to a familiar concept or past experience, it is more easily accepted. The mind can more readily process and internalize suggestions with existing associations.

Example

A hypnotist might suggest that the feeling of warmth from sunlight is similar to the warmth of confidence. By associating confidence with the familiar, pleasant sensation of sunlight, the suggestion is more likely to be accepted.

Application

Altering Hypnosis to Suggestibility

Understanding whether a person is more physically or emotionally suggestible helps hypnotists choose the most effective techniques and suggestions.

Physically Suggestible Individuals

Hypnotists might use more concrete, sensory, and direct suggestions for these individuals.

Effective Techniques

  • Progressive relaxation
  • Arm levitation
  • Direct physical suggestions
  • Sensory-focused imagery

Emotionally Suggestible Individuals

Hypnotists might use more abstract, emotional, and indirect suggestions for these individuals.

Effective Techniques

  • Visualization
  • Metaphorical language
  • Emotionally charged narratives
  • Indirect suggestion methods

By recognizing and adapting to an individual's type of suggestibility, hypnotists can enhance the effectiveness of their sessions and achieve better therapeutic outcomes.
